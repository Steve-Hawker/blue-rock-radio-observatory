## Personal Note

M1 is the observer's favourite object. A weekend observing session at
Alston Observatory (UCLan) with Mark Norris, Jason Kirk, and Derek
Ward-Thompson included an experiment to calculate the age of M1 from
expansion data. Using 10 nodes the derived explosion date was 1093 AD —
39 years from the accepted 1054 AD date. A satisfying result demonstrating
that sparse but well-chosen data points can give reliable answers — a
principle directly applicable to the Blue Rock observing programme.

---

## Why M1 is Different from Cas A

Cas A is a classical shell remnant — expanding debris from the explosion,
decreasing in flux as the shell thins. M1 is powered by the Crab Pulsar,
a neutron star of ~1.4 solar masses rotating 30 times per second. The
pulsar continuously injects energy into the surrounding nebula, maintaining
its brightness and producing emission across the entire electromagnetic
spectrum from radio to gamma ray.

The Crab Pulsar luminosity is ~100,000× the total luminosity of the Sun,
almost entirely in radiation. This continuous energy injection is why M1
is stable while Cas A declines — and why M1 is one of the most studied
objects in astrophysics across all wavelengths.

## Role in Blue Rock Programme

### Secondary calibration standard
At ~1040 Jy M1 is detectable with the Blue Rock system. As a secondary
calibration check it provides independent validation of the flux scale
derived from Cas A. Agreement between M1-derived and Cas A-derived system
temperatures confirms the calibration is consistent.

### Personal science target
The observer's interest in M1 makes it a natural inclusion. A detected
spectrum and flux measurement compared with published values is a
satisfying personal result alongside the primary science programme.

### Age calculation connection
The weekend session at Alston Observatory demonstrated the age calculation
methodology from expansion data. A radio flux measurement of M1 at Blue
Rock connects the observer's optical/computational experience of the object
with its radio properties — the same object viewed through a different
instrumental window.

## Detection Feasibility

With Blue Rock SEFD ~138,000 Jy and M1 flux ~1040 Jy:

Using the radiometer equation for SNR = 5:
- Bandwidth: ~2 MHz (continuum observation)
- Required integration: ~45 minutes for SNR = 5

Detectable in a single session. Not as quick as Cas A (~seconds) but
entirely practical as a dedicated science session.

Note: M1 is a continuum source at 1420 MHz — the observation measures
total flux density, not a spectral line. Different observing mode from
HI observations — no Doppler correction required, wider bandwidth,
shorter integration needed.

## Notes

The Crab Pulsar pulses at 30 Hz — far too fast to detect with standard
spectral integration times. Not relevant for flux measurement observations
but worth noting as context for the emission mechanism.

M1 is unresolved by the Blue Rock 17° beam. Treat as a point source
for all flux calculations.

Declination +22° means M1 transits 15° lower than Cas A and M31 from
San Jose. Still well above all site obstructions at 55° elevation.
