M81 at declination +69° is nearly circumpolar from San Jose —
available for most of the day year-round. It never sets below 30°
elevation making it a genuinely year-round target, unlike M31 and
M33 which are seasonal.

Transit elevation of ~58° — lower than M31 and M33 but comfortably
clear of all site obstructions.

## Why M81 is a Stretch Goal

M81 is significantly more distant than M31 and M33 — at 3.7 Mpc
it is ~5× further than M33 and ~4.7× further than M31. Its HI flux
of ~25 Jy km/s is comparable to M33 despite being further away —
it is a larger and more HI-rich galaxy.

However the detection challenge is real:

- HI flux ~25 Jy km/s spread across ~440 km/s velocity width
- Peak flux per channel at 1 km/s resolution: ~25/440 ≈ 0.057 Jy/channel
- Lower than M33 on a per-channel basis
- Requires very long co-added integration

M81 is classified as a stretch goal — achievable in principle over
four years of observation but not guaranteed. It is observed
opportunistically during summer months when M31 is unavailable.

## The M81/M82 Interacting Pair

M81 and M82 (the Cigar Galaxy) form an interacting pair — M82 is
a starburst galaxy being tidally disrupted by M81. Both lie within
the 17° Blue Rock beam when pointing at M81, meaning M82 emission
may contribute to the observed spectrum.

M82 HI properties:
- Systemic velocity: ~203 km/s (receding)
- HI mass: depleted by interaction — much less HI than M81
- Angular separation from M81: ~0.6°

At 17° beamwidth both galaxies are captured simultaneously. Their
different systemic velocities (-34 km/s for M81, +203 km/s for M82)
mean their HI profiles are separated by ~237 km/s in velocity space
and do not overlap — both can be identified independently in a single
spectrum. This is scientifically interesting — a two-galaxy spectrum
from a single pointing.

## Expected Spectral Profile

M81 at 59° inclination produces a moderate double-horn profile.
Systemic velocity of -34 km/s means the HI line is very slightly
blueshifted — essentially at the rest frequency:

$$f_{obs} \approx 1420.405 \times \left(1 + \frac{34}{299792}\right) \approx 1420.57 \text{ MHz}$$

The full velocity range (-254 to +186 km/s LSR) spans approximately
2.1 MHz. Care required with LO offset — at LO = 1422 MHz the M81
profile sits between -1.43 and +0.57 MHz in baseband, close to the
DC spike region. May require LO adjustment to 1420 MHz for M81
observations to keep the profile clear of DC artefacts.

M82 would appear at approximately +203 km/s LSR — about +0.96 MHz
from the HI rest frequency, or at -0.44 MHz in baseband at LO = 1422 MHz.

**Note:** LO offset strategy for M81 observations requires careful
planning — document in session logs.

## Interaction with INV001 Noise Budget

At 3.7 Mpc M81 is the most distant target in the programme and the
most demanding in terms of sensitivity. Detection feasibility depends
critically on the actual system SEFD derived from Cas A observations.

If SEFD is significantly higher than the theoretical ~138,000 Jy
(e.g. due to higher than expected LNA noise or RFI environment),
M81 may become impractical within the four-year programme.

Conversely, if the Airspy R2 upgrade and improved RFI flagging
reduce effective SEFD, M81 becomes more achievable.

The INV001 noise budget analysis (to be completed after commissioning)
will determine whether M81 is a realistic target or aspirational only.

## Role in Blue Rock Programme

M81 is the programme's most ambitious extragalactic target. Its role:

- **Science:** HI detection at 3.7 Mpc demonstrates the outer range
  of the instrument's capability
- **Bonus science:** Simultaneous M82 detection possible in same beam
- **Year-round availability:** fills summer observing schedule when
  M31 and M33 are unavailable at night
- **Sensitivity benchmark:** M81 detection (or meaningful upper limit)
  characterises the programme's ultimate sensitivity after years of
  co-addition

A non-detection of M81 after four years is not a failure — a well-
documented upper limit on HI flux is a legitimate scientific result
that characterises the instrument's sensitivity envelope.
